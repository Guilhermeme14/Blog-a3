Dell, Intel and University of Cambridge deploy the UK’s fastest AI supercomputer

Dell, Intel, and the University of Cambridge have jointly unveiled the Dawn Phase 1 supercomputer, a cutting-edge AI supercomputer in the UK. This supercomputer, part of the UK AI Research Resource (AIRR), fuses AI and high-performance computing technologies to tackle significant global challenges. It brings the UK closer to the exascale computing threshold and is powered by Dell PowerEdge XE9640 servers and Intel Data Center GPU Max Series accelerators. Dawn's applications span various domains, including healthcare, engineering, green fusion energy, climate modeling, cosmology, and high-energy physics. The collaboration aims to deliver a Phase 2 supercomputer in 2024 with tenfold performance improvements. This advancement signifies the UK's commitment to scientific and AI research, showcasing a powerful partnership between academia and industry. The technical details and performance metrics of Dawn Phase 1 will be revealed at the Supercomputing 23 (SC23) conference in mid-November.

UK paper highlights AI risks ahead of global Safety Summit

The UK Government has released a comprehensive paper addressing the capabilities and risks associated with frontier AI. UK Prime Minister Rishi Sunak emphasized the need for an honest dialogue about AI, highlighting both unprecedented opportunities and significant dangers it presents. The report focuses on generative AI, which underlies popular chatbots and image generation software, and envisions a future in which terrorists could exploit AI to plan biological or chemical attacks, raising serious concerns about global security. The document addresses security risks associated with generative AI up to 2025 and explores uncertainties in the development of frontier AI up to 2030. The report discusses the possibility of AI-driven cyber-attacks becoming faster, more effective, and on a larger scale by 2025. The AI Safety Summit aims to facilitate discussions on addressing frontier AI risks, including concerns related to misuse by non-state actors in cyberattacks or the design of biological weapons.

'I thought I would never play an instrument again'

Christine Williamson's life took a drastic turn when she suffered a brain aneurysm, resulting in the loss of function on her left side. She feared that her days of playing musical instruments were over, but her outlook changed when she joined a special music research program. This program, run by the local charity Brain Injury Matters in Northern Ireland, utilizes Virtual Reality (VR) to enable disabled musicians to play specially designed virtual instruments. The initiative is a collaboration between Queen's University Belfast and Drake Music Northern Ireland.

Castle Hill Hospital's virtual reality kit used to help patients recover

Castle Hill Hospital in Cottingham, East Yorkshire, is reportedly the first hospital in the UK to use a virtual reality (VR) treadmill for brain injury rehabilitation. This innovative equipment aims to revolutionize the way patients exercise and offers immersive experiences like shopping trips or mountain treks. Patients can walk, jog, or run in any direction on the VR treadmill, which adapts to their movements and provides a high degree of freedom and fluidity. Dr. Abayomi Salawu, a rehabilitation medicine consultant, sees this as a groundbreaking addition, making rehabilitation engaging and successful by turning traditional exercises into enjoyable, immersive activities that simulate real-life scenarios. It also allows for the design of tailored exercise programs for patients.
